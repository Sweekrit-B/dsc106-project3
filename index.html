How Do Different Climate Models Predict Changes in Global and Regional Vegetation Carbon Storage?

Sweekrit Bhatnagar (sbhatnagar@ucsd.edu), Nirav Kanthed (nkanthed@ucsd.edu), and Arah Sanders (a4sanders@ucsd.edu)

Plants absorb CO₂ from the atmosphere and store it as carbon in their biomass, a process directly related to the amount of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere and the climate conditions that they are exposed to. This visualization shows how vegetation carbon mass (measured in kilograms of carbon per square meter, kg C m⁻²) is projected to change from 2015 to 2100 under three different climate scenarios, using the BCC-CSM2-MR climate model from the CMIP6 project. Each map shows the anomaly, which represents how much carbon stored in plants has shifted compared to the 2015 baseline.

This data was procured by downloading monthly vegatation biomass outputs from the CMIP6 archive by querying the data catalog for the appropriate variable (cVeg), model (BCC-CSM2-MR), and scenarios (SSP2-4.5, SSP3-7.0, SSP5-8.5). The data was then processed by calculating anomalies relative to the 2015 baseline, aggregating monthly outputs into 5-year averages, and merging neighboring 2° × 2° latitude–longitude grid cells to emphasize large-scale spatial trends.

Green regions indicate an increase in vegetation carbon storage compared to 2015, suggesting that plants are accumulating more biomass, potentially driven by elevated atmospheric carbon dioxide levels and enhanced plant growth. This chart compares the global mean vegetation carbon anomaly over time for each emissions scenario relative to the 2015 baseline. Although all scenarios initially show modest increases in vegetation carbon, their trajectories diverge later in the century as warming intensifies. Moderate warming can temporarily enhance plant growth through longer growing seasons and increased atmospheric CO₂ concentrations, but stronger warming and climate stressors — including heat extremes, drought, wildfire activity, and ecosystem degradation — can reduce the ability of vegetation to continue storing carbon. By 2100, higher-emissions pathways exhibit larger departures from the baseline and greater variability, highlighting how future climate conditions may alter the role of terrestrial ecosystems in the global carbon cycle.

Writeup

Our primary goal for this project is to create an interactive visualization exploring the projected change of vegetation carbon mass (cVeg). To this end, we have created three interactive maps and one line graph to show the change of cVeg under three different climate response scenarios (SSP2-4.5, SSP3-7.0, SSP5-8.5) from 2015 to 2100. Following is our design rationale.

In terms of interactive controls:

  1. A slider for the year selection, allowing users to scrub through time and compare how different emission pathways lead to different outcomes across the globe. We chose to use a year slider because it provides an intuitive way for users to explore the temporal dimension of the data and see how vegetation carbon storage evolves over time under different climate scenarios. In comparison to a dropdown menu or buttons for specific years, a slider allows for smoother transitions and a more engaging user experience, enabling users to easily observe trends and patterns in the data.
  2. We considered controls for coarsening the data (merging neighboring grid cells) and aggregating the data into custom multi-year bins. However, we ultimately decided against adding these controls to keep the interface simple and focused on the main interactive element (the year slider). We wanted to avoid overwhelming users with too many options and instead provide a clear and straightforward way to explore the data. Furthermore, recomputing the visualizations with user-selected coarsening and binning would require significant additional processing time and could lead to performance issues, especially since our default visualizations are already pre-computed for optimal performance.
  3. We added a dropdown menu for users to select specific continents to focus on, which updates the maps and line graph accordingly. This feature allows users to explore regional variations in vegetation carbon storage and understand how different parts of the world may be affected differently by climate change. We considered using a world map with clickable regions to select focus areas, but ultimately decided on a dropdown menu for simplicity and ease of use, especially since coarse latitude and longitude squares make bordering regions difficult to distinguish.
  4. To account for this lack of customization from the region dropdown, we added a brush tool to allow users to select specific regions of the map and see the corresponding data in the line graph. This feature enables users to explore localized trends and patterns in vegetation carbon storage, providing a more detailed understanding of how specific areas may be impacted by climate change.
  5. In the line graph, users can click on specific points to jump to the corresponding year in the maps and also select a continent to focus on through the dropdown menu, which updates both the maps and the line graph to reflect the selected region. This adds consistency and interactivity between the different visualizations, allowing users to easily navigate between the global trends shown in the line graph and the spatial patterns shown in the maps.

In terms of graphs and visual encodings:

  1. We chose a choropleth map to visualize the spatial distribution of vegetation carbon anomalies across pre-defined grid cells, as this type of map effectively conveys spatial patterns and variations in the data. Each grid cell is colored based on the magnitude of the anomaly from 2015, allowing users to easily identify areas of increase or decrease in vegetation carbon storage.
  2. Our color scheme uses green to indicate areas where more carbon is stored in vegetation compared to 2015, while red indicates areas where less carbon was stored. This choice was made due to common associations of green with growth and health (as an increase in plant biomass is generally beneficial for pulling heightened CO2 levels from the atmosphere) and red with decline or danger (as a decrease in plant biomass can lead to more CO2 remaining in the atmosphere, further amplifying global warming). We considered other, more contrasting, color schemes, but decided against them due to the lack of association and effectiveness in conveying the intended message without cognitive overload. Additionally, we included blue to represent oceans where data is unavailable and yellow to indicate areas with no significant change from the baseline, providing clear visual cues for interpreting the maps.
  3. We included a shared legend below the maps to explain the color encoding and provide the scale for the anomalies, ensuring that users are easily able to identify the meaning of the colors and the range of values represented.
  4. We included a line graph showing the global mean carbon biomass in vegetation (cVeg) anomaly over time for each scenario to provide a clear representation of the overall trend for a region. This allows users to be able to quantify the change they see in the maps and understand numerically how much the vegetation carbon storage changes relative to the 2015 baseline in each scenario. We considered using a bar chart or scatter plot to show the global mean cVeg anomaly, but ultimately decided on a line graph because it effectively conveys trends over time and allows for easy comparison between scenarios.

Overall, our design choices were guided by the goal of creating an engaging and informative visualization that allows users to examine spatial and temporal trends in vegetation carbon storage under different climate scenarios, while keeping the interface intuitive and focused on the key interactive elements.
