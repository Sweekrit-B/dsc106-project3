How Do Different Climate Models Predict Changes in Global and Regional Vegetation Carbon Storage?

Plants absorb CO₂ from the atmosphere and store it as carbon in their biomass, a process directly related to the amount of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere and the climate conditions that they are exposed to. This visualization shows how vegetation carbon mass (measured in kilograms of carbon per square meter, kg C m⁻²) is projected to change from 2015 to 2100 under three different climate scenarios, using the BCC-CSM2-MR climate model from the CMIP6 project. Each map shows the anomaly, which represents how much carbon stored in plants has shifted compared to the 2015 baseline.

This data was procured by downloading monthly vegatation biomass outputs from the CMIP6 archive by querying the data catalog for the appropriate variable (cVeg), model (BCC-CSM2-MR), and scenarios (SSP2-4.5, SSP3-7.0, SSP5-8.5). The data was then processed by calculating anomalies relative to the 2015 baseline, aggregating monthly outputs into 5-year averages, and merging neighboring 2° × 2° latitude–longitude grid cells to emphasize large-scale spatial trends.

The maps illustrate changes in the amount of carbon stored in vegetation relative to the 2015 baseline under three different climate scenarios. Green regions indicate an increase in vegetation carbon storage compared to 2015, suggesting that plants are accumulating more biomass, potentially driven by elevated atmospheric carbon dioxide levels and enhanced plant growth. In contrast, red regions indicate a decline in vegetation carbon storage relative to 2015, suggesting vegetation loss or dieback that releases stored carbon back into the atmosphere, further amplifying global warming.

Mean Change in Vegetation Carbon (kg C m⁻²) Relative to 2015 Until 2100, Focusing on

This chart compares the global mean vegetation carbon anomaly over time for each emissions scenario relative to the 2015 baseline. Although all scenarios initially show modest increases in vegetation carbon, their trajectories diverge later in the century as warming intensifies. Moderate warming can temporarily enhance plant growth through longer growing seasons and increased atmospheric CO₂ concentrations, but stronger warming and climate stressors — including heat extremes, drought, wildfire activity, and ecosystem degradation — can reduce the ability of vegetation to continue storing carbon. By 2100, higher-emissions pathways exhibit larger departures from the baseline and greater variability, highlighting how future climate conditions may alter the role of terrestrial ecosystems in the global carbon cycle.
